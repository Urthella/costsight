## Numbers to memorize for Q&A

These are from the run that generated the committed `examples/comparison.csv`:

| Detector | Overall F1 | Point Spike F1 | Level Shift F1 | Gradual Drift F1 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| STL                | **0.80** | 0.50 | **0.68** | **0.77** |
| Z-Score            | 0.11 | **1.00** | 0.00 | 0.00 |
| Isolation Forest   | 0.34 | 0.24 | 0.27 | 0.21 |

Likely exam questions and short answers:

- **"Why is Z-Score F1 so low overall?"** Because it's a stationary
  baseline — a level shift moves the rolling mean *with* the data, so the
  z-score never spikes. That's the textbook limitation we wanted to show.
- **"Why does STL's point-spike precision look low (0.33)?"** Six of
  STL's flagged days are at the boundary of the drift / level-shift
  windows. We count those as false positives because they're outside the
  injected anomaly day, but they're not "wrong" in any operational sense.
- **"Why doesn't Isolation Forest dominate?"** Because the per-service
  cost series is essentially univariate — IF's edge is multi-feature
  separation, which we partially recover with engineered features
  (lag ratios, season residual, trend slope) but cannot fully simulate
  on a single cost column.
